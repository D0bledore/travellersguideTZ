Facts about Tanzania

Tanzania, located in East Africa, is known for its diverse culture, rich history, and natural beauty. The country gained independence from Britain in 1961. The capital city is Dodoma, while Dar es Salaam remains the largest city and main economic hub. The official languages are Swahili and English, and the currency is the Tanzanian shilling (TZS).

Tanzania's vibrant and youthful population stands at 67,438,106 in 2023, experiencing a robust annual growth rate of 2.96% and holding a significant global presence at 0.84%, ranking 22nd in the world. Life expectancy brings a mix of hope and challenge, with an average of 67.6 years, where women live slightly longer at 69.8 years compared to 65.5 years for men. The heart-wrenching infant mortality rate is 29.5 deaths per 1,000 live births, and the mortality rate for children under five is 39.7 per 1,000, highlighting ongoing healthcare struggles. Urbanization is steadily advancing, with 38% of the population living in cities, and Dar es Salaam thrives as the bustling heart of Tanzania, home to 2,698,652 people.

History of Tanzania

Tanzania's history is deeply rooted in ancient human activity, highlighted by the discovery of the Paranthropus boisei skull by Mary Leakey at Olduvai Gorge in 1959, dating back to between 2.3 and 1.2 million years ago. Before the 19th century, most historical records focus on the coastal areas, although the interior holds significant prehistoric sites like Olduvai. The coastal regions saw major waves of in-migration and trade from Greece, Rome, Phoenicia, Arabia, Persia, and India starting as early as the 5th century BCE. These early trading interactions led to the establishment of numerous Asian and Arab settlements along the coast and into the interior, fostering relatively friendly relations between Arabs and African neighbors. The arrival of the Portuguese in the late 15th century challenged Arab dominance, but their influence waned by the early 18th century due to alliances between coastal Arabs and the ruler of Muscat.

The late 18th century saw renewed interest in the region from the French, who revived the slave trade from Kilwa, prompting the sultan of Muscat to appoint a new Omani governor there. This period marked a significant increase in Arab trading activities, particularly in slaves and ivory, with Arab traders penetrating deeper into the interior by the 19th century. Important trading centers like Tabora and Ujiji emerged, though they faced resistance from African leaders like Mirambo, who disrupted trade routes in the 1860s and '70s.

European interest in Tanganyika grew in the 19th century with missionaries such as Johann Ludwig Krapf and Johannes Rebmann reaching Kilimanjaro in the late 1840s. Their reports stimulated British explorers Richard Burton and John Hanning Speke to journey from Bagamoyo to Lake Tanganyika in 1857-58, with Speke also exploring Lake Victoria. David Livingstone's expedition in 1866 aimed to expose the slave trade's horrors and promote legitimate commerce, leading to further explorations by H.M. Stanley and V.L. Cameron. These European explorations, driven by a mix of geographic curiosity and humanitarian concerns, significantly influenced the region, attracting more missionary societies and intertwining Tanzania's history with broader global narratives.

Source: Britannica - History of Tanzania

Bagamoyo, a historic town in Tanzania, played a pivotal role in the East African slave trade during the 18th and 19th centuries. The town's coral buildings, characteristic of Swahili construction, served as a gathering point where slaves from the African interior were brought by native, Arab, and Portuguese traders before being shipped to Zanzibar. Zanzibar, under Omani control by the mid-17th century, became a major hub for supplying slave labor to local plantations and European colonies. The architectural heritage of Bagamoyo reflects its diverse influences, including grand houses built by wealthy Arabs and Indian merchants and the distinct styles introduced by German settlers in the 19th century. Today, the significance of Bagamoyo and the Central Slave and Ivory Trade Route is recognized, with efforts underway to preserve its historic buildings and raise public awareness about its past.

Source: World Monuments Fund - Bagamoyo Historic Town

Geography of Tanzania

Tanzania is the largest country in East Africa, covering an area of 947,303 square kilometers. It features a varied landscape that includes coastal plains, the Great Rift Valley, and the central plateau. The country is home to Africa's highest peak, Mount Kilimanjaro, and numerous lakes, including Lake Victoria, the largest lake in Africa, and Lake Tanganyika, one of the deepest lakes in the world.

Tanzania's diverse geography also includes the Serengeti Plains, which are famous for the annual migration of over a million wildebeest and hundreds of thousands of zebras. The country's extensive coastline along the Indian Ocean is dotted with idyllic beaches and coral reefs, making it a popular destination for both wildlife enthusiasts and beach lovers.

Source: Nations Online - Tanzania

Tourist Attractions in Tanzania

Tanzania offers a wealth of tourist attractions, from natural wonders to cultural experiences. The Serengeti National Park is famed for its annual migration of over 1.5 million wildebeest and hundreds of thousands of zebras. The Ngorongoro Crater, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is a stunning natural amphitheater teeming with wildlife. Additionally, the idyllic Zanzibar Archipelago offers pristine beaches, historic Stone Town, and vibrant local markets.

Other notable attractions include Mount Kilimanjaro, the tallest free-standing mountain in the world, which draws climbers and adventurers from around the globe. The Selous Game Reserve, one of the largest faunal reserves in the world, is another key highlight, offering a remote and unspoiled wilderness experience. For those interested in history and culture, the ancient ruins of Kilwa Kisiwani and the Bagamoyo town provide a glimpse into Tanzania's storied past.

Source: Tanzania Tourism
